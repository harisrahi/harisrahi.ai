The Pattern Across All Four Workstreams

The Contractor produces every artifact. The CMs observe, validate, shadow, attend, and receive explanations. The knowledge transfer language, "walking CMs through," "sharing best practices," "explaining rationale," describes presentation, not capability building.

This structure follows the conventional model for technology engagements built around traditional software delivery. But AI capability doesn't transfer the same way. Tools not owned by the team freeze the moment the engagement ends. Static documents become outdated. Neither produces a team that can build what comes next.

The key question is whether Google wants to buy artifacts that depreciate or capability that compounds.

The current scope assumes AI capability is something that gets delivered. It isn't. It's something that gets built by the people who will use it, on the work they actually do, under expert direction and methodology-based review.

Phase 1: Foundation Fluency & Workflow Modernization

CMs bring their actual work into the engagement, not hypothetical scenarios.

What Happens

Up to 15 Category Managers bring their actual work into the engagement. Their real workflows, their real pain points, their real deliverables. Under expert direction, the CMs learn how to think critically about where AI creates leverage in their work. They analyze their own workflows, not a report a contractor handed them. They learn to prompt effectively, understand how to challenge AI outputs, and identify when AI is helping versus when it's producing confidently wrong outputs.

Then they build. Each CM constructs working AI assistants on Google's platform stack, applied to their actual workflows.

Contract Drafting & Review

Drafts, redlines, and flags risk against organizational standards and precedent

Built by CMs

Bid & Proposal Analysis

Extracts vendor commitments, compares proposals side-by-side, surfaces gaps and inconsistencies

Built by CMs

RFx Development

Accelerates requirements drafting, ensures completeness, improves clarity for vendors

These are starting points, not endpoints. As the CMs work through their own workflows, use-case-specific assistants will emerge, tools unique to how Google's Category Management operates that no outside contractor could anticipate or predefine.

What CMs Walk Away With

Working tools they built, understand, and can modify. Not tools someone else built and explained to them. When a workflow changes, when priorities shift, when AI platforms evolve, the CMs can adapt what they built. That's the difference between owning capability and renting it.

What the Contractor Delivers

The structured methodology and curriculum tailored to Google's context. A workflow opportunity assessment identifying where AI creates the highest leverage. Capability evaluations with clear visibility into which CMs are advancing, where gaps exist, and who shows aptitude for more advanced development. Methodology-based review of every assistant the CMs produce.

Phase 2: Augmented Execution & Applied Development

Foundation training creates literacy. Proficiency develops when it's applied to live work.

What Happens

Phase 2 builds directly on Phase 1. The CMs apply their new capability to active category management work. The Contractor provides structured expert guidance and review: reviewing CM-built tools in live workflow context, identifying brittle solutions before they fail, guiding CMs toward more sophisticated AI augmentation as literacy develops into fluency.

This is also where the AI tools portfolio expands and matures. The tools built in Phase 1 get tested against live workflow context. Some will work as designed. Some will need refinement. Some will reveal entirely new opportunities. Phase 2 is designed so that a core group of CMs develop into sustained builders within the organization.

What CMs Walk Away With

A validated, workflow-tested portfolio of AI solutions built and refined by the team. Changed workflows already in daily use. The confidence and capability to build the next tool, and the foundation to determine whether advanced development warrants a deeper engagement.

What the Contractor Delivers

Ongoing capability assessments documenting team progression. A final readiness report evaluating team capability, tool maturity, and a recommendation on whether and how to proceed to advanced development. Methodology documentation retained by Google for continued internal use.

Phase 3: Advanced Development (Follow-On Engagement)

Phase 3 is not included in this SOW. It is described here to show the full capability arc and to frame why the sequencing matters.

A subset of CMs will demonstrate aptitude that goes beyond assistant-building. These individuals can learn to develop sophisticated, production-quality AI applications, the work the current SOW attempts in Workstreams C and D. But that work requires practitioners who have proven foundation fluency through augmented execution on real work. Skipping to production development produces operators, not builders.

A separate SOW would be scoped after Phase 1/2 completion, contingent on demonstrated CM aptitude and sufficient real-world validation of Phase 1/2 tools in live operating conditions.

Proposed Revised Scope: Services and Deliverables

Contractor will provide the following Services and Deliverables to Google. The engagement is structured in two phases for a cohort of up to 15 Category Managers. In each phase, CMs are the primary builders; Contractor provides expert direction, structured methodology, and methodology-based review.

Phase 1: Foundation Fluency & Workflow Modernization

Scope

CMs develop foundational AI fluency by analyzing their own workflows and building working AI assistants on Google's platform stack (Vertex AI, ADK, Gems, Google Enterprise Agents, NotebookLM, etc.), under Contractor's expert direction and methodology-based review.

Services

  1. 1.Design and deliver a structured engagement methodology and curriculum tailored to Google's Category Management context, workflows, and platform stack.
  2. 2.Direct CMs through a systematic analysis of their existing workflows, tools, and pain points, providing the analytical framework and guiding CMs to identify and prioritize AI modernization opportunities themselves.
  3. 3.Instruct CMs in AI fundamentals, prompt engineering, critical evaluation of AI outputs, and assistant development, anchored to their actual workflows and deliverables, not hypothetical scenarios.
  4. 4.Direct CMs in the design and development of a portfolio of working AI assistants, including category-agnostic assistants (Market & Supplier Research, Contract Drafting & Review, Bid & Proposal Analysis, RFx Development) and use-case-specific assistants identified during the workflow analysis.
  5. 5.Conduct methodology-based review of all CM-built assistants to assess rigor, reliability, and alignment with Google's standards.
  6. 6.Evaluate CM capability at a defined checkpoint, assessing fluency progression, adoption patterns, and aptitude for advanced development.

Contractor Deliverables

1. Engagement Methodology & Curriculum The structured methodology, curriculum, and instructional materials tailored to Google's CM context.
Contractor's underlying methodology is pre-existing intellectual property, licensed for Google's use in this engagement.
2. Workflow Opportunity Assessment A documented analysis of identified AI modernization opportunities across CM workflows, scored against an impact/feasibility framework developed and applied by the CMs under Contractor direction.
3. Phase 1 Capability Evaluation An assessment of each CM's fluency progression, adoption speed, and readiness for Phase 2, with identification of gaps and recommended focus areas.

CM Outputs

Produced by CMs under Contractor direction and methodology-based review
1. Working AI Assistant Portfolio A set of functional, CM-built AI assistants deployed on Google's platform stack, covering both category-agnostic and use-case-specific applications.
2. Workflow Analysis & Redesign Documentation CM-authored documentation of analyzed workflows, identified opportunities, and redesigned processes incorporating the AI assistants they built.

Phase 2: Augmented Execution & Applied Development

Scope

CMs apply and extend their Phase 1 capability on live Category Management work. Contractor provides structured expert guidance to help CMs mature CM-built tools in live workflow context, advance toward more sophisticated applications, and assess the team's ability to operate independently.

Services

  1. 1.Provide structured expert guidance and review as CMs apply their AI assistants to active Category Management deliverables and workflows.
  2. 2.Review and stress-test CM-built tools in live workflow context, identifying brittle solutions, missed opportunities, and areas for refinement.
  3. 3.Guide CMs toward more sophisticated applications as fluency matures, including advanced prompting techniques, multi-step workflows, and complex use cases beyond Phase 1 scope.
  4. 4.Conduct ongoing capability assessments documenting team progression, tool maturity, and emerging gaps.
  5. 5.Evaluate and identify CMs demonstrating aptitude for advanced AI application development (potential Phase 3 candidates).

Contractor Deliverables

1. Ongoing Capability Assessments Documented evaluations of CM progression, tool performance in live conditions, and team readiness at defined checkpoints.
2. Final Readiness Assessment A comprehensive assessment of team capability, validated tool portfolio, and a recommendation on Phase 3 readiness, including identification of high-aptitude CMs and proposed scope for advanced development.
3. Methodology Reference Materials Documentation of the engagement methodology, frameworks, and instructional approach, retained by Google for continued internal use and scaling.

CM Outputs

Produced by CMs under Contractor direction and methodology-based review
1. Validated & Expanded Assistant Portfolio The Phase 1 assistant portfolio, refined through real-world application and expanded with additional assistants developed during Phase 2.
2. Production-Tested Workflow Documentation CM-authored documentation of workflows operating with AI assistants in live workflow context, including performance observations and refinement history.

Phase 3: Advanced Development

Phase 3 is not included in this SOW. It is referenced to define the full capability arc. Phase 3 would cover advanced AI application development by high-aptitude CMs identified in Phase 2, including production-quality builds, multi-agent orchestration, and complex workflow automation.

A separate SOW would be scoped upon completion of Phase 2, informed by the Final Readiness Report, and contingent on (a) demonstrated CM aptitude in Phases 1 and 2, and (b) sufficient real-world validation of Phase 1/2 tools in live operating conditions.

Independent Contractor Compliance

This section addresses how the proposed engagement structure aligns with independent contractor classification requirements under applicable federal and state standards. It is provided for reference in internal review and is not a legal opinion.

Independent contractor classification is evaluated across three dimensions: behavioral control, financial control, and relationship type. The proposed engagement satisfies the key indicators on all three.

Behavioral Control

Who determines how the work is performed?

The Contractor brings a pre-existing, proprietary methodology developed independently and prior to the engagement. The Contractor determines how instruction is designed and delivered, how workshops are structured, how capability is assessed, what analytical frameworks are applied, and the sequence in which the engagement progresses. The client defines the desired outcomes. The Contractor determines how those outcomes are achieved.

The Contractor does not receive task-level direction from client management, does not follow client-prescribed processes, and does not operate under client supervision in determining the approach to the work.

Financial Control

Is the Contractor operating as an independent business?

The Contractor operates through a separately formed legal entity, provides all equipment necessary to perform the services, and has a core methodology that is pre-existing intellectual property developed at the Contractor's own investment and risk, not created at the client's direction or expense. This methodology is licensed for use, not assigned.

The Contractor is compensated against defined deliverables and milestones, bearing the risk of performance against those commitments. The Contractor is free to offer services to other clients and is not economically dependent on a single engagement.

Relationship Type

What is the nature and duration of the engagement?

The proposed engagement has a defined scope structured in two distinct phases, each with specific deliverables, defined completion criteria, and a clear end state. Phase 1 concludes with a documented Capability Evaluation. Phase 2 concludes with a Final Readiness Report. The engagement has a defined term. It is not open-ended.

The Contractor is not integrated into the client's management hierarchy, does not report to a client manager for daily work direction, and does not function as a member of a client team. The Contractor operates as an external expert engaged to deliver a defined outcome within a bounded period.
